Pedro Martinez -- The G.O.A.T?

Pedro Martinez was one of the most dominant pitchers in baseball. His prime was in the late 90s and early 2000s, the height of the steroid era. In that environment, Pedro was simply unhittable, and put offenses to a screeching halt every time he went up to the mound. It’s not outlandish to say this, but I’m going to make a case for Pedro Martinez being the greatest pitcher of all time. The goat (G.O.A.T), if you will.

Now, I took a random list from a Google search “Greatest Pitchers of All Time” and just chose one. All of these pitchers are generally going to be on this list, especially the modern ones such as Pedro, Kershaw, Ryan, and Johnson. They’re undisputed kings of baseball. We’ll look at two stats between these consensus pitchers: ERA and ERA+, each being the average of the player’s career. ERA being Earned Run Average, which indicates the average runs scored when a pitcher is on the mound. ERA+ is the same stat, adjusted to the player’s ballpark/run environment, where 100 is average.

A huge jump on the chart for Pedro. He had a relatively high ERA on that list comparatively but remember Pedro was pitching at the height of the steroid era. Still, he wasn’t first on that list. It was Clayton Kershaw, an active pitcher for the LA Dodgers. He’s pretty good too. Let’s look a little closer.

So, let’s take the 2019 pitchers who had above 80 innings pitched, meaning that they were a significant part of the team either as a reliever or a starter. Then, let’s stick Clayton Kershaw in there, but at his peak 2013 season. Let’s also put Pedro Martinez in there at his peak 1999 season. We’ll look at four stats: ERA, ERA+, FIP, and SO/9. You already know about ERA and ERA+, so I’ll skip the explanation. Let’s talk about FIP though. FIP stands for Fielding Independent Pitching, which just looks at the true outcomes between a batter and a pitcher: A walk, strikeout, or hit. It then uses that to calculate the ERA. An ERA that isn’t reliant on your team. Nice. SO/9? Well, the average strikeouts a pitcher acquires over 9 innings. So, let’s see what we get.

Clayton had more production as far as inning pitched, and he did have a monstrous ERA of 1.83, but Pedro outperformed him in every other metric, which is unbelievable considering 1999 was, again, the height of the steroid era. These aren’t all tell-all stats, but it’s still unbelievably impressive. We’ll take a look at one more chart before you go.

I forgot to mention, Pedro Martinez is in the Baseball Hall of Fame, he was inducted in 2015. We’ll be looking at a chart that has every Hall of Famer’s ERA and WAR. We already know all about ERA, but let’s go over WAR, or Wins Above Replacement. It essentially calculated the value of a player to their team, using the metric “How many wins could we get if we replaced this guy?” Average WAR in the Hall of Fame for pitchers is 69, the average ERA is 3.00. I’m not making that up.

What strikes me the most about this chart is just many of the other Hall of Famers have so many more innings pitched that Pedro, yet Pedro was still more productive based on WAR, and we already know his ERA is below the average, his being 2.93. Many of the other pitchers on this list could be considered stat accumulators, as nobody in the future will likely ever reach those numbers due to the increasing physicality and limiting pitch counts on pitchers. It’s hard to claim the goat in anything, it takes a lot of cherry-picking and hoping others agree with you. Still, pitching in the steroid era and getting these types of numbers is no easy task. I don’t think any man could do it. Maybe a goat could though. I like to think there’s one named Pedro Martinez.
